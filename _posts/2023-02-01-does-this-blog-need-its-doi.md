In case you missed it, in the end of December (2022),
a researcher who studies medieval manuscript provenance 
demonstrated in a series of blog posts how he had been plagiarized 
by another researcher at a Swiss institute.
(I didn't miss it, 
because the Twitter algorithm showed me 
amazing threads like 
[this one](https://twitter.com/paularcurtis/status/1608146988511690753)
from [Dr. Paula R. Curtis](http://prcurtis.com/))
The researcher who was *not* plagiarizing,
Peter Kidd, showed in these posts 
how material in a book by the other researcher, 
Professor Carla Rossi, 
was clearly plagiarized 
from posts on his blog focused on research.
Soon a cadre of 
"Extremly Online Medieval Studies researchers"
(as they're described in a nice write up  
[here](https://thecritic.co.uk/receptiogate-and-the-absolute-state-of-academia/)).
did a lot of internet sleuthing 
and brought down an entire house of cards.
It turned out the same researcher had plagiarized other works,
and the institute was little more than a fake website 
whose only other verifiable employee was the researcher's husband.

Notably, an email sent to Kidd 
from the supposed secretary of Rossi,
who we now think was actually just Rossi,
said the following:

> "I regret to inform you that blogs are not scientific texts, published by academic publishers, so their value is nil!"

The whole incident made me think about
how we don't have a lot of tools that help people cite blogs.
(Please don't think I'm saying tech tools are magic bullets that fix broken systems!)
To you, that might sound like a non-sequitur,
but it turned out that I'm not the first one to think about this 
(as is usually the case).
In this post--which has its own Digital Object Identifier, 
so you can, in theory, cite it and have someone navigate 
to my post you're citing later--I'll save you some Googling 
and share what I found out.
Foreshadowing: most of what I found out is that 
a DOI won't save us from plagiarism,
or even do much else,
but there are plenty of people that would like 
better ways to cite things,
including blog posts.
