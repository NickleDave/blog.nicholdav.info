title:  "Artificial intelligence in 13 easy steps: on Braitenberg's Vehicles"
---

I have just finished reading Vehicles by Valentino Braitenberg,
after someone mentioned it at [the Learning Salon](https://www.learningsalon.ai/) many moons ago.
(You can find the book on the [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/g), here:
<https://archive.org/details/vehiclesexperime00brai >)
I want to write about the book and why reading it excited me.
What is the book about?
You can get a sense from the subtitle,
"Experiments in Synthetic Psychology".
There's a lot to unpack in each of those three nouns,
"experiments", "synthetic", and "psychology".
Somehow Braitenberg pulls a philosophical hat-trick,
making us grapple with the definition of each of these terms in one little book.
And by doing so,
he inspires me, making me want to do experiments with synthetic psychology.
Having a meaning for those terms,
I want to explore that meaning,
in a way that I never really felt for "artificial intelligence".
More and more I feel like we'll look back and realize
it was a mistake to imagine
that intelligence could exist in an artificial, disembodied form,
a brain in a jar that we pour on problems when we need them solved on demand.

How did Braitenberg manage all this, in my mind?
I'll give some quotes here to sum up his message,
but first let me better explain the content of the book.
The main body of the text consists of a list of vehicles.
With each sucessive vehicle,
Braitenberg adds some functionality to the machine.
The first vehicle is simply a motor wired to a sensor
that is driven by positive feedback
towards that which it senses;
by the time we get to the last vehicle,
we have a machine that seems to exhibit
all the richness of human behavior
that we love to idealize
as the highest form of intelligence:
memory, language, consciousness.

And so Braitenberg's conceit is to force us
to reconsider the terms in his subtitle,
by just asking us to
run some thought experiments with his vehicles.
The glee is apparent in his voice
as he adds each new feature
and teases us that the vehicle now seems to be
exhibiting some behavior that we would describe
with a term from psychology.
From just the first two, we get "alive", "fearful", and "aggressive".

This is his agenda then,
to force us to abandon what he defines as the analytical approach
taken by psychology,
and to instead take what he calls a synthetic approach.
We have all these facts about the nervous system, he says,
and we have ideas about how they add up to something, to behavior.
He goes beyond this though, and in a sort of offhanded way
makes what might be an even stronger claim.
He seems to suggest that if this synthetic approach really works,
then we should be able to run experiments in reality
to test our ideas. Near the end of the book he says,
"Most ideas can be translated into computer programs
and thus are easily put to the experimental test."
I love that Braitenberg is completely unconcerned
with whether there's a difference between a computer simulation
and an experiment.
I think he's exactly right.
All experiments are simulations;
it's just that some are cheaper than others.
(Yeah I know you want me to reference that Feynman quote
about "What I cannot create, I do not understand".
Not gonna do it. Okay I just did.)
